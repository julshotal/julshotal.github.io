The Living Dead

Hover to see the effects of radium on the human body

Amelia Malia “Mollie” Maggia's pain began with a single tooth falling out.(3) Then came the excruciating gum and jaw pain, leading her to seek treatment with dentist Dr. Joseph Knef.(6) During one of these treatments, after slight pressure broke her jawbone, Dr. Knef removed her entire lower jaw "...not by an operation, but merely putting his fingers in her mouth and lifting it out."(4)

Another former radium dial painter, Grace Fryer, visited a dentist regarding growing jaw pains. Radiographs showed the jawbone honeycombed with holes, the bone decaying.(2) As more and more girls came forward with mouth pains and similar bone deterioation a singular trend arose. Every single one had once been or were currently employed with U.S. Radium.(2)

June 3, 1923. Helena Quinlin, 21, dies from mouth uleraction, tissue death in her throat and mouth, and infection spreading from her gums
July 15, 1923. Irene Rudolph, 21, dies due to complete jaw necrosis.(3)

☢

Mollie Maggia died September 12, 1922 at the age of 24 from a massive hemorrhage, the infection that had by now crept into her neck disintegrating her jugular vein.(3)

Her death, and the ailments of many of the other workers beginning to show symptoms of radium poisoning, was attributed to syphilis.(2)

In 1927 doctors discovered that some of Grace Fryer's vertebrae were crushed, and that her spine had essentially collapsed from bone sarcoma.(4) She could no longer sit up or walk without a back brace. (3)

Grace Fryer died on October 27, 1933 at 34 (4)
